    UT Gymnastics

    The art of unnatural positions

    At the beginning of my freshman year of high school, I saw videos of "street ninjas" and decided that I did not want to simply wish I could be like those people in the video and I sought out the local parkour group in Houston to learn their craft. Over the next 4 years, I slowly began transitioning from training parkour outdoors to training parkour inside gymnastics gyms. At the beginning of my freshman year of college, I saw that the university had a gymnastics team and I went to them with the hopes of being allowed to train in their gym. After observing the highly motivated and close-knit team for a week, I decided to tryout for the UT gymnastics team. I have been training with the team for about 2 years and I plan to compete still rings during the competition season in my third year.

    Research

    The age of (scientific) exploration

    My career in research started with my enrollment in the University of Texas' Freshman Research Initiative (FRI) program. I joined a laboratory stream working with dendrimer-encapsulated nanoparticles for catalysis and learned the fundamentals of a chemical and organic chemical laboratory, while also engaging in scientific writings and presentations. During my second year at university, I became a part of the clinical research team under Dr. Michael Singer and began working in data collection, writing for publications, and even structuring my own research. I believe that scientific research is the most rewarding activity since the reward is indubitably favorable for human progress, longevity, and quality of life.

    Music

    The Auditory Art

    My introduction to music was a hesitant one in 6th grade as I was coerced into joining orchestra (like many others) by my parents. While I resisted at first, orchestra quickly became the most anticipated part of my day. I have played violin religiously since that time 9 years ago. The most important lessons I learned, however, were not about music, but about complementary human interactions. I learned that it is vastly more fruitful to have a group of harmonious musically mediocre individuals that function together, than an uncoordinated group of exceptional individuals. I have assembled an ensemble at the University of Texas in order to keep music perpetually present in my life by performing pieces in public for universal enjoyment.
